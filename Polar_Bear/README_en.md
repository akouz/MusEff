# Polar Bear

The Gilbert cell is the basis of analog multipliers and transconductance amplifiers. This overdrive uses an extremely simplified cell. The signal is applied to the base of transistor Q1A and simultaneously to the emitter of transistor Q2A. As a result, the collector of transistor Q1A produces a signal multiplied by itself, i.e., raised to the power of two. If a sine wave is applied to the input, a signal of double the frequency is generated at the collector of Q1A. A mixture of the input signal and the squared signal is generated at the collector of Q1B.

For small signals, distortion is proportional to the amplitude of the input signal. To achieve greater distortion, two cells are connected in series. The output signal of the first cell is attenuated by divider R16 and R17 so that the signal level at the input of the second cell is equal to the signal level at the input of the first. Then, both cells contribute equally to distortion.

The sound is reminiscent of a three-stage tube amplifier. In the example shown, the gain control is at 9:00 for half the time and at 15:00 for the other half. The signal was recorded to the PC line input and was not processed.
